Spaghetti Tower

Build the tallest tower that stands on its own.

Engineer the tallest freestanding tower you can using only dry spaghetti and marshmallows.

  • 20 sticks of dry spaghetti
  • Half a bag of mini marshmallows
  • Ruler
  • Timer
  • A flat surface
  1. Plan a wide, triangular base for stability.
  2. Join lengths of spaghetti using marshmallows as connectors.
  3. Build upwards, keeping the structure balanced.
  4. Measure the height of the freestanding tower.
  5. Rebuild to beat your record.

This is structural engineering. Triangles are rigid and barely deform under load, which is why bridges and cranes use trusses made of them. A wide base lowers the centre of gravity for stability. Spaghetti is strong when pushed (compression) but snaps easily when bent, so good designs keep the struts short and straight.

Spaghetti can splinter — handle it gently and don't eat it afterwards.

A self-supporting tower; strong designs beat 30–50 cm.

Paper Bridge Load Test

Find out how shape beats material.

Discover how the shape of a structure — not just what it's made of — decides how much weight it can hold.

  • Several sheets of A4 paper
  • Two stacks of books (supports)
  • Coins or small weights
  • Ruler
  1. Lay one flat sheet across a 15 cm gap and add coins until it collapses — record the number.
  2. Fold a fresh sheet into a zig-zag (concertina) and test again.
  3. Try rolling sheets into tubes to use as beams.
  4. Compare how many coins each design holds.
  5. Design and test your strongest bridge.

Folds and corrugations dramatically increase a beam's stiffness by raising its 'second moment of area' — the same reason steel I-beams and corrugated cardboard are so strong for their weight. As you load the bridge, the top is squeezed (compression) and the bottom is stretched (tension).

Watch out for coins falling off the edge.

A folded bridge that holds many times the weight of a flat sheet.

Balloon-Powered Car

Build a car driven by escaping air.

Engineer a small car that powers itself across the floor using nothing but the air escaping from a balloon.

  • Balloon
  • Plastic bottle or card for the chassis
  • 4 wheels (bottle caps or card discs)
  • 2 skewers or straws for axles
  • Tape
  • A straw for the nozzle
  1. Make the chassis and tape on straws to hold the axles.
  2. Push skewers through with wheels on the ends — make sure they spin freely.
  3. Tape a straw into the balloon's neck and fix it to the body, pointing backwards.
  4. Blow up the balloon through the straw, then let go.
  5. Race it and tune the design.

This demonstrates Newton's third law: the balloon pushes air backwards, and the air pushes the car forwards with an equal and opposite force called thrust — exactly how a rocket works. Reducing weight and friction in the axles makes the car travel further, a lesson in iterative design.

No hazards — just clear a runway.

A car that shoots forward on its own; tuned ones travel several metres.

Build a Water Filter

Turn muddy water clear, layer by layer.

Engineer a layered filter — like a miniature water-treatment plant — that turns muddy water clear.

  • Plastic bottle cut in half
  • Cotton or coffee filter
  • Sand
  • Small gravel
  • Activated charcoal
  • Muddy water
  • Cup
  1. Invert the top half of the bottle (cap off) into the bottom half to make a funnel.
  2. Layer from the bottom up: cotton, then charcoal, then fine sand, then gravel.
  3. Slowly pour muddy water on top.
  4. Watch clearer water drip into the cup below.
  5. Experiment with different layer orders.

This is environmental engineering. Each layer traps different sizes of dirt: gravel catches the big bits, sand catches fine particles, and activated charcoal adsorbs many dissolved chemicals and odours onto its huge internal surface area. Real treatment plants use the same staged filtration.

Important: the filtered water looks clear but is NOT safe to drink — filtering does not kill germs.
