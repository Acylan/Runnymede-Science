Density Rainbow

Stack liquids into a tower that refuses to mix.

Layer everyday liquids of different densities into a clear glass to build a colourful tower where nothing blends together.

  • Tall clear glass
  • Honey
  • Washing-up liquid
  • Water + food colouring
  • Vegetable oil
  • Coloured rubbing alcohol
  • A spoon
  1. Pour honey into the centre of the glass without touching the sides.
  2. Slowly add washing-up liquid on top.
  3. Tilt the glass and pour coloured water down the side over the back of a spoon.
  4. Add vegetable oil the same gentle way.
  5. Finish with coloured rubbing alcohol on top.
  6. Let it settle and watch the bands form.

Density is mass divided by volume. Denser liquids sink and less dense liquids float, so each one settles into its own layer — honey at the bottom, alcohol on top. Because these liquids don't dissolve into each other quickly, the boundaries stay sharp.

Rubbing alcohol is flammable — keep it away from flames, and don't drink any layer.

Cabbage pH Indicator

Brew a liquid that changes colour for acids and bases.

Turn red cabbage into a colour-changing 'magic' liquid that reveals whether a substance is an acid or a base.

  • Red cabbage
  • Hot water
  • Jar + strainer
  • Clear cups
  • Lemon juice, vinegar, baking-soda water, soap, plain water
  1. Chop the cabbage and soak it in hot water for about 10 minutes.
  2. Strain off the purple liquid — that's your indicator.
  3. Pour a little into several clear cups.
  4. Add a different test substance to each cup.
  5. Record the colour each one turns.

Red cabbage contains a pigment called anthocyanin that changes shape depending on pH. Acids turn it pink or red, neutral solutions stay purple, and bases turn it blue, green or yellow. It's a completely natural pH indicator.

Hot water can scald — ask an adult to help. Don't taste any of the test cups.

A rainbow from red (acidic) through purple (neutral) to green/yellow (basic).

Elephant Toothpaste

A foaming eruption powered by a catalyst.

Create a giant column of warm foam by dramatically speeding up the breakdown of hydrogen peroxide.

  • Plastic bottle
  • 6% hydrogen peroxide
  • Warm water
  • Dry yeast
  • Washing-up liquid
  • Food colouring
  • Tray
  • Goggles + gloves
  1. Stand the bottle in a tray to catch the mess.
  2. Add peroxide, a squirt of soap and some food colouring.
  3. In a cup, mix the yeast with a little warm water.
  4. Pour the yeast mixture into the bottle.
  5. Stand back and watch.

Hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) naturally breaks down into water and oxygen. Yeast contains an enzyme called catalase — a catalyst that makes this happen almost instantly. The soap traps the escaping oxygen as thousands of bubbles, creating foam. The reaction also gives off heat, so the foam feels warm.

Wear goggles and gloves — 6% peroxide irritates skin and eyes. Do this with an adult. It is NOT real toothpaste; don't put it in your mouth.

Strawberry DNA

Pull real DNA out of a strawberry.

Extract real, visible strands of DNA from a strawberry using only kitchen ingredients.

  • Strawberry
  • Zip-lock bag
  • Washing-up liquid
  • Salt
  • Water
  • Coffee filter
  • Cup
  • Cold rubbing alcohol
  • Skewer
  1. Mix 1 tsp soap, a pinch of salt and half a cup of water.
  2. Put the strawberry in the bag, add the liquid and mash for a minute.
  3. Filter the mush through a coffee filter into a clean cup.
  4. Slowly pour cold alcohol down the side of the cup.
  5. Watch white DNA strands appear, then lift them with a skewer.

Soap breaks open the cell and nuclear membranes (which are made of fat), releasing the DNA inside. Salt helps the DNA strands clump together. DNA doesn't dissolve in alcohol, so it falls out of solution as visible white threads. Strawberries are octoploid — they carry eight copies of each chromosome — so they're packed with DNA.

Rubbing alcohol is flammable and not for drinking. Wash your hands afterwards.
